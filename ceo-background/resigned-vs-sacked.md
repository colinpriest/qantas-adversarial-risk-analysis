## Joyce's Remuneration: Resign vs. Removed

Alan Joyce's contract contained **malus and clawback provisions** on both short-term incentives (STI) and long-term incentive plan (LTIP) shares, but these were triggered by the *board's discretion* based on performance/conduct findings — not by whether he resigned or was [fired.abc](http://fired.abc)+1

The key financial difference in Joyce's case:

- **Resignation / "retirement" (what happened):** Joyce brought forward his planned November 2023 departure to **5 September 2023**. He departed as a "good leaver," retaining eligibility for pro-rata vesting of outstanding LTIP performance rights (2022–2024 and 2023–2025 plans), plus vested but unreleased 2021–2023 LTIP shares. His initial total FY23 remuneration was reported at **$21.4 million**.livewiremarkets+1
- **Board subsequently applied clawback:** In August 2024, the board used the malus/clawback provisions to forfeit 100% of 2021–2023 LTIP shares ($8.36 million) and cut his STI by 33% (~$900K), reducing his payout by **$9.26 million** to roughly **$14.4 million** — still on top of ~$125 million earned over his 15-year tenure and a $16.85 million share sale in June 2023.theguardian+2[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1TlIkxD_MVA)]​
- **Had he been terminated for cause:** Under typical ASX executive contracts, termination for serious misconduct usually results in **forfeiture of all unvested incentives** (no pro-rata vesting) and potentially no notice-period payment. The board explicitly found **"no deliberate wrongdoing"**, which meant the full "termination for cause" provisions were not [triggered.qantasnewsroom.com](http://triggered.qantasnewsroom.com)+2

In practical terms, by resigning before any formal finding of misconduct, Joyce preserved his "good leaver" status, which kept alive his entitlement to pro-rata LTIP vesting on the 2022–2024 and 2023–2025 plans. The board could (and did) claw back a portion, but a "bad leaver" / termination-for-cause scenario would likely have meant **total forfeiture** of all outstanding incentives rather than a partial reduction. He also collected a final $3.8 million LTIP payout in September [2025.abc.net](http://2025.abc.net)+2

## Behavioural Research on Preferring Resignation over Dismissal

There is a well-developed body of research on this, though it tends to be framed in terms of **stigma avoidance**, **face-saving**, and **labour market signalling** rather than a formal utility function:

## The "Retired or Fired" Ambiguity

The Stanford Closer Look study by **Gow, Larcker, and Tayan (2017)** developed the **Push-out Score™** (0–10 scale) to estimate the likelihood a CEO was pressured to leave. Examining ~1,400 Russell 3000 turnover events (2017–2021), they found:gsbpreserve.stanford+2

- Only **23%** of departures scored as clearly voluntary; **29%** scored as likely involuntary; the remaining **48%** fell in the ambiguous middle (scores 4–7).beckershospitalreview+1
- CEOs rarely admit to being fired. Both the board and the departing CEO have **mutual incentives** to frame the exit as voluntary — the board avoids admitting a governance failure, and the CEO avoids stigma.exechange+1
- Post-departure behaviour differs: voluntarily departing CEOs are more likely to **join the company's board or retire**; those with high push-out scores are more likely to seek **new CEO/executive roles elsewhere** — consistent with needing to rehabilitate their [career.clsbluesky.law](http://career.clsbluesky.law).columbia+1

## Evaluative Stigma from Forced Departure

- **Semadeni et al. (2008)** and **Sutton & Callahan (1987)** found that leaders from failed organisations face **demotion in subsequent employment** and reduced board appointments — effects driven by **evaluative stigma** (external observers attribute the failure to the leader personally).[[mail.tristanbotelho](https://mail.tristanbotelho.com/files/BotelhoMarx2025EmployeeFailureEmployeeCareers_Preprint.pdf)]​
- **Bilinski & Novak** found that executives at stigmatised firms demand a **compensation premium** to offset impaired job mobility and social costs, and that when they leave, they move to **less prestigious firms**, take longer to find new roles, and accept **lower pay**.[[openaccess.city.ac](http://openaccess.city.ac)]​
- A 2025 study on **CEO turnover and director reputation** found that directors involved in **forced CEO turnovers** experience persistent increases in withheld votes at other boards — suggesting the stigma of firing/being fired radiates to associated parties.[[corpgov.law.harvard](http://corpgov.law)]​

## The Face-Saving Dynamic

- **Problematic Departures** (Dalarna University research) specifically analyses the **euphemisms** used in CEO departure announcements — "personal reasons," "mutual agreement," "retirement" — and argues these serve a **face-saving** function for both parties, allowing the CEO to preserve social identity and the board to avoid signalling governance failure.[[uu.diva-portal](https://uu.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:161761/FULLTEXT01.pdf)]​
- The **Exechange** data confirms that companies and executives "have motives not to reveal forced turnovers as such" and that "anonymous sources… follow their own interest and may be inclined to give biased information."[[exechange](https://exechange.com/history)]​

## Quantitative Preference Evidence

While there is no single paper that estimates a formal utility function for "resign vs. be fired," the quantitative evidence consistently shows:

- **Labour market penalty for forced exits is large:** CEOs with high push-out scores experienced **-42% shareholder returns** in the three years prior (vs. +8% for voluntary), and their subsequent career paths are measurably worse.[[beckershospitalreview](https://www.beckershospitalreview.com/hospital-management-administration/reading-between-the-lines-when-ceos-abruptly-step-down/)]​
- **Mutual incentive to disguise:** Nearly half of all departures (48%) fall in the ambiguous zone, suggesting systematic efforts to obscure involuntary exits.gsb.stanford+1
- **Stock price volatility increases with push-out score**, meaning investors react more dramatically to departures that look involuntary — creating an additional incentive for both parties to keep the score low.[[corpgov.law.harvard](http://corpgov.law)]​

In Joyce's case, the pattern fits the literature precisely: an accelerated "retirement" framed as succession planning, with the board deferring accountability findings to a post-departure governance review, allowing both parties to avoid the immediate stigma and financial consequences of a termination-for-cause.ceoworld+2
