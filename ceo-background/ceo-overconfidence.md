## Part 1: Empirical Evidence of Joyce's Self-Confidence

Several observable behaviours from Joyce's tenure provide concrete, quantifiable indicators of what the academic literature would classify as overconfidence or hubris:

## Share Sale Timing

Joyce sold over 90% of his Qantas shareholding on 1 June 2023, netting ~$14.8 million, just **three days** after Qantas provided the ACCC with a spreadsheet detailing 10,000+ cancelled flights at the centre of the "ghost flights" investigation. He sold shares he'd bought at $1.50 for $6.74. This timing implies confidence that either (a) the ACCC matter would not materially damage the stock, or (b) personal financial optimisation outweighed reputational risk — behaviour consistent with the Malmendier & Tate framework of executives who hold concentrated positions based on inflated beliefs about their firms, then act decisively when their private calculus [shifts.australianaviation.com](http://shifts.australianaviation.com)+2

## Pattern of Doubling Down Under Pressure

- **2011 Fleet Grounding:** Joyce grounded the entire Qantas fleet worldwide to force a resolution of union disputes — an unprecedented and extraordinarily high-stakes gambit. He defended this as the only rational response, framing ongoing union action as an existential threat costing $15 million/week.[[abc.net](http://abc.net)]​
- **Outsourcing 1,700 ground staff (2020):** The High Court ultimately ruled this illegal, but Joyce maintained it was a necessary survival decision "staring into the abyss," defending it publicly as late as 2025.[[travelweekly.com](http://travelweekly.com)]​
- **Response to ACCC ghost flights:** Qantas's initial response was to argue the ACCC's position was overstated, and Joyce's public comments minimised the scope of the [issue.abc](http://issue.abc)+1
- **Staying despite accumulating crises:** Joyce originally planned to retire in November 2023 and only brought this forward to September 2023 after the reputational damage became undeniable. Multiple sources noted he "did not want to go out this way."[[sbs.com](http://sbs.com)]​[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cr-WT6kugoo)]​

## The Governance Review's Own Finding

The Saar governance review found **"excessive deference toward Joyce, who'd overcome previous crises"** and that **"decisions weren't sufficiently challenged."** This is a direct description of what Hayward & Hambrick (1997) call the hubris feedback loop: past success → media/board praise → inflated self-assessment → reduced challenge → riskier decisions.fortune+3

## Compensation as a Self-Importance Proxy

Hayward & Hambrick (1997) use the **CEO pay slice** — CEO compensation relative to the next-highest-paid executive — as a measure of self-importance. Joyce's $21.4 million FY23 compensation was several multiples of other Qantas executives, and he had accumulated ~$125 million over his 15-year tenure. The sheer scale of his bonus-heavy structure, combined with his willingness to defend it publicly, is consistent with what the literature treats as a revealed preference for self-assessed high value.theguardian+2

## Part 2: Quantitative Behavioural Research on Executive Overconfidence

There is a deep, well-quantified literature. The key streams are:

## Malmendier & Tate: Options-Based Overconfidence (the foundational measure)

The most influential quantitative measure of CEO overconfidence comes from **Malmendier & Tate (2005)**, published in the *Journal of Finance*. Their **"Longholder" measure** classifies a CEO as overconfident if they hold vested stock options that are at least **40% in-the-money** through to expiration, rather than exercising them early (as a rational, risk-averse, underdiversified agent should).eml.berkeley+2

Key quantitative findings:

- Overconfident CEOs (Longholder = 1) show **investment–cash flow sensitivity** that is significantly higher than non-overconfident CEOs, meaning they overinvest when internal funds are available.econweb.ucsd+1
- This effect is robust across multiple measures of overconfidence, including press portrayal (how often the CEO is described as "confident" vs. "cautious" in major outlets).onlinelibrary.wiley+1
- A follow-up (*Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 2015) confirmed that overconfident CEOs are more acquisitive and that their acquisitions destroy more shareholder value.[[aeaweb](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fjep.29.4.37)]​

## Hayward & Hambrick: Hubris and Acquisition Premiums

In a sample of 106 large acquisitions, **Hayward & Hambrick (1997)** found four indicators of CEO hubris — recent firm performance, recent media praise, CEO self-importance (pay relative to #2 executive), and a composite — are all **positively and significantly** associated with the size of acquisition premiums paid. The greater the hubris, the greater the shareholder losses. Critically, the relationship is **strengthened when board vigilance is weak** (high proportion of inside directors, CEO is also board chair).studeersnel+2

## The "Better-Than-Average" Effect

The foundational psychology finding: in Svenson's (1981) classic study, **93% of American drivers** rated themselves in the top 50% for driving skill, and over **80%** placed themselves in the top 30%. This extends to executives: **March & Shapira (1987)** found that most managers believe they take risks more wisely than their colleagues, perceive risk as largely controllable, and attribute this to their own skills and [information.www2.um.edu](http://information.www2.um.edu)+2

## CEO Overconfidence Meta-Analysis

A 2023 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Management* integrated findings across multiple literature streams and found:[[journals.sagepub](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01492063221110203)]​

- A **positive main effect** of CEO overconfidence on firm performance (overconfident CEOs do take on more productive risk, on average)
- But this operates **through strategic risk-taking**, and the variance of outcomes is much wider — consistent with overconfident CEOs generating both big wins and big losses
- The three facets of overconfidence — **overestimation** (inflated self-appraisal), **overplacement** (believing one is better than peers), and **overprecision** (excessive certainty) — all contribute.[[journals.sagepub](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01492063221110203)]​

## CEO Narcissism and Board Deference

A meta-analysis by **Asad (2021, CBS)** covering 67 studies and 121,748 observations found that narcissistic CEOs are viewed favourably by stock markets (especially in high power-distance cultures) but are paradoxically associated with **both** corporate social responsibility and corporate irresponsibility. This aligns with the Joyce pattern — championing marriage equality and the Voice referendum (CSR) while simultaneously presiding over illegal outsourcing and ghost flights (irresponsibility).research-api.cbs+1

The narcissism–overconfidence interaction is significant: **Salehi et al. (2021)** found that both CEO narcissism and overconfidence have **positive and significant** effects on corporate risk-taking, reinforcing each other.[[emerald](https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/tqm-07-2020-0168/full/html)]​

## The "Lake Wobegon Effect" at CEO Level

Recent McKinsey research confirms that **CEOs consistently rate their own performance higher** than both their direct reports and boards rate them, at all career stages. Board directors also tend to overestimate CEO performance relative to direct reports, creating a feedback loop that reduces candid challenge — directly paralleling the Saar review's finding of "excessive deference" toward Joyce.[[linkedin](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/marvinscout_seeing-ceo-blind-spots-activity-7376498953128722433-w-Ic)]​
