## 2) Legal Consequences of a Second Strike

Under Sections 250U–250W of the *Corporations Act 2001*, the consequences are: [investsmart.com](https://www.investsmart.com.au/investment-news/what-is-the-two-strikes-rule/32539)

- A **conditional spill resolution** must be put to the same AGM at which the second strike occurs. [thymac.com](https://www.thymac.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2016/10/Two_strikes_executive_remuneration_February2013_-1.pdf)
- The spill resolution requires a **simple majority (>50%)** of eligible votes to pass. KMP and closely related parties cannot vote on the remuneration report or the spill resolution, but **can vote on director re-elections**. [guerdonassociates](https://www.guerdonassociates.com/articles/two-strikes-and-investor-behaviour/)
- If the spill resolution passes, a **spill meeting** must be held within **90 days**. [www8.austlii.edu](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/cth/consol_act/ca2001172/s250w.html)
- At the spill meeting, **all directors who were on the board** when the relevant directors' report was approved **cease to hold office** immediately before the end of the spill meeting — **except the managing director/CEO**, who may continue to hold office indefinitely under ASX Listing Rules. [ato.gov](https://www.ato.gov.au/law/view/document?LocID=%22PAC%2F20110042%2FSch1-Cl13%22)
- Resolutions to appoint persons to the vacated offices are put to vote at the spill meeting. [www8.austlii.edu](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/cth/consol_act/ca2001172/s250w.html)
- If the company **fails to hold the spill meeting** within 90 days, each person who is a director at the end of those 90 days commits a **strict liability offence**. [www8.austlii.edu](http://www8.austlii.edu.au/cgi-bin/viewdoc/au/legis/cth/consol_act/ca2001172/s250w.html)
- The company need not hold the spill meeting if all relevant directors have already resigned before the 90-day deadline. [www5.austlii.edu](https://www5.austlii.edu.au/au/legis/cth/consol_act/ca2001172/s250w.html)

**What does NOT happen:**

- Directors are **not automatically removed**. They only vacate their offices if the spill resolution passes AND a spill meeting is held. [investsmart.com](https://www.investsmart.com.au/investment-news/what-is-the-two-strikes-rule/32539)
- A second strike alone does not change executive pay, forfeit bonuses, or alter contractual entitlements. [fool.com](https://www.fool.com.au/2023/12/09/10-popular-asx-stocks-that-faced-shareholder-revolts-this-agm-season/)

# Analysis of Australian AGM Remuneration Second Strikes and Corporate Accountability: 2020–2023

## The Regulatory Framework and the Philosophy of the Two-Strikes Rule

The Australian corporate governance environment is defined by a unique and robust legislative mechanism known as the "two-strikes" rule, which was formally integrated into the Corporations Act 2001 in July 2011. This regime was established to address a perceived lack of accountability regarding executive remuneration and to empower shareholders with a significant, albeit non-binding, voice in the governance of publicly listed companies. The core of the mechanism lies in Section 250R, which requires a company’s remuneration report to be put to a non-binding vote at the Annual General Meeting (AGM). While the advisory nature of the vote allows the board to retain ultimate decision-making power, the "two-strikes" provision introduces a escalating consequence for repeated dissent.

A "strike" is technically recorded when 25% or more of the votes cast on the resolution to adopt the remuneration report are against the proposal. This 25% threshold represents a significantly lower bar than the 50% required for most ordinary resolutions, reflecting the legislature's intent to allow even a substantial minority of shareholders to signal serious dissatisfaction. If a company receives such a strike, it is legally required to explain in its subsequent annual report the actions it has taken in response to shareholder concerns or the reasons why no action was taken.

The stakes increase dramatically in the second year. If a company receives a "second strike"—another vote of 25% or more against the remuneration report—at the following year's AGM, a "spill resolution" must be put to the shareholders. If this spill resolution passes with a simple majority of 50%, the company must hold a "spill meeting" within 90 days. At this meeting, all directors who were in office when the directors' report was approved, excluding the managing director, must stand for re-election. This "board spill" is the ultimate sanction, theoretically capable of removing an entire board of directors in response to remuneration-related grievances.

In practice, the 2020–2023 period has demonstrated that while strikes are common, successful board spills remain non-existent. This suggests that the two-strikes rule serves primarily as a powerful communication tool and a "pressure valve" for shareholder frustration. It forces a level of engagement between boards and institutional investors that might not otherwise occur, compelling directors to justify their pay structures against the backdrop of company performance and broader social expectations.

## Macro Trends and Economic Catalysts (2020–2023)

The period from 2020 to 2023 represents one of the most volatile stretches in the history of the Australian "Say on Pay" regime. The era was bookended by the global COVID-19 pandemic and the subsequent "cost-of-living" crisis, both of which fundamentally altered shareholder expectations regarding executive compensation.

### The Pandemic Disruption and "JobKeeper" Sensitivity (2020–2021)

In 2020, the onset of COVID-19 created an environment of extreme uncertainty. While the total number of strikes remained steady at 40, the severity of the votes was often muted as shareholders initially adopted a "wait and see" approach while companies navigated lockdowns. However, a specific point of friction emerged: the payment of executive bonuses by companies that had simultaneously received government support via the "JobKeeper" program. Investors viewed this as a breach of social contract, leading to high-profile strikes at companies where the board failed to exercise downward discretion.

By 2021, this sensitivity evolved into a broader scrutiny of "regulatory gravity." Large financial institutions and resources companies found themselves in the crosshairs not necessarily for their pay quantum, but for their social license to operate. Rio Tinto, though not a second strike in 2021, received a massive 61% protest vote following the destruction of the Juukan Gorge rock shelters, demonstrating that shareholders were increasingly using the remuneration report as a proxy for general governance failures.

### The Rise of the "Retention Grant" and Technical Dissent (2022)

The 2022 season was characterized by a "return to normalcy" in strike numbers (22 in the ASX 200), but a sharp increase in the technical sophistication of the dissent. As the global economy reopened, companies faced an intense "war for talent," leading many boards to issue one-off "retention grants" or "sign-on bonuses" to keep or attract key executives.

Proxy advisors and institutional investors were highly critical of these awards, labeling them "pay for pulse". The second strikes at Goodman Group and Lovisa were directly tied to these practices. Shareholders argued that retention should be a byproduct of a well-structured LTI plan, not an additional layer of guaranteed pay that bypasses performance hurdles. This period also saw the lowest level of average support for remuneration reports since 2017, as investors pushed back against what they perceived as "opportunistic" adjustments to pay frameworks.

### The 2023 Record Surge and the Cost-of-Living Backlash

The 2023 AGM season was a watershed moment, recording 41 strikes across the ASX 300—the highest number since the two-strikes rule began. The average "against" vote in strike situations spiked to 45.7%, a significant increase from the 34.2% seen in 2022.

The primary catalyst was the stark disconnect between soaring executive payouts and the economic hardship of the average shareholder. High-profile strikes at Qantas (83% against) and Harvey Norman (82% against) became cultural flashpoints. At Qantas, the $21.4 million payout to former CEO Alan Joyce, against a backdrop of litigation and operational dysfunction, was seen by 83% of shareholders as entirely unjustified. This record-breaking year for dissent signaled that "quantum" was once again a primary concern, but it was quantum coupled with a perceived "tone-deafness" from boards.

## Deep Dive: Root Causes of Shareholder Dissent

Analysis of the second-strike data from 2020 to 2023 reveals three recurring themes that trigger repeated shareholder revolts: misalignment of pay and performance, lack of disclosure transparency, and the inappropriate use of board discretion.

### Misalignment of Pay and Performance (TSR Dominance)

The single strongest predictor of a remuneration strike is a poor Total Shareholder Return (TSR). Institutional investors have consistently argued that if shareholders are losing value, executives should not be receiving maximum or even median bonus payouts. At Lake Resources, the second strike in 2023 was a direct consequence of a share price that had plummeted by over 80%, while executive pay remained substantial.

Statistical modeling by proxy advisors confirms that TSR has a significant negative effect on oppositional voting; as TSR increases, the likelihood of a strike decreases. However, boards often find themselves at odds with this market-driven metric, arguing that executives should be rewarded for achieving operational milestones—such as the completion of a feasibility study or a successful M&A—even if the market sentiment remains bearish. This tension is particularly acute in the materials and technology sectors, where commercial progress may lag behind share price movements by years.

### The "Disclosure Gap" and STI/LTI Transparency

A lack of transparency regarding Short-Term Incentive (STI) and Long-Term Incentive (LTI) targets is a frequent driver of "first strikes" that often matures into a "second strike" if not addressed. Shareholders are increasingly resistant to "opaque" non-financial metrics. When a board claims a CEO has met 100% of their "strategic objectives" but provides no detail on what those objectives were or how they were measured, investors tend to vote "no" as a matter of principle.

Link Administration's second strike in 2022 was partially attributed to poor disclosure surrounding the hurdles for one-off awards. Similarly, Fortescue Metals faced intense scrutiny in 2023 for the "repeated use of discretion to provide special recognition awards" without clear, disclosed criteria. The insight here is that shareholders do not necessarily object to high pay, but they do object to high pay that they cannot verify against rigorous benchmarks.

### Board Discretion: The "Safety Net" Controversy

The use of "upward discretion"—where a board chooses to pay a bonus even when a financial hurdle has not been met—is perhaps the most inflammatory action a board can take. In 2023, Treasury Wine Estates received a strike because shareholders were displeased with the board's exercise of discretion to allow the vesting of LTIs despite missing financial targets.

Conversely, the failure to exercise "downward discretion" is also a major trigger. In cases involving workplace fatalities or significant regulatory breaches, shareholders expect boards to use "malus" or "clawback" provisions to reduce executive payouts. The 28% "no" vote at Woolworths in 2023 was explicitly linked to the board's failure to sufficiently penalize executive bonuses following the deaths of two staff members. This reflects a shift in governance standards where safety is no longer viewed as a "tick-box" exercise but as a fundamental performance metric.

## Case Studies of Board Response Strategies

The effectiveness of the two-strikes rule is best measured by the actions boards take between the first and second strike. The 2020–2023 data highlights a stark divide between "responsive" and "resistant" boards.

### The Responsive Strategy: Goodman Group and Lake Resources

Goodman Group serves as a model for a board attempting to navigate the two-strikes rule through incremental reform. Following a first strike in 2021 driven by concerns over LTI quantum, the board engaged in a massive consultation process and subsequently reduced the quantum of LTI awards by 10%. While the company still incurred a second strike in 2022—reflecting the deep-seated nature of institutional concerns—the board's willingness to make structural changes was noted by proxy advisors as a positive step toward alignment.

Lake Resources similarly attempted a responsive path. After its 2022 strike, the company introduced a malus and clawback policy and completely reworked its CEO's pay structure to strengthen the link between incentives and project milestones. The 50% "no" vote in 2023 was less a rejection of these changes and more a protest against the 80% share price decline and the resignation of multiple key executives. This highlights a "second-order" insight: corrective actions can be technically perfect, but they cannot save a board from a strike if the underlying business performance is catastrophic.

### The Resistant Strategy: Lovisa and Dicker Data

A different pattern emerges among founder-led or founder-influenced boards. Lovisa Holdings and Dicker Data have each received four and five consecutive strikes, respectively. In these cases, the boards have largely remained "resistant" to shareholder feedback.

Founder-led boards often argue that their remuneration structures are tailored to a specific growth phase that traditional proxy advisor models fail to understand. For example, Lovisa’s board defended high sign-on bonuses as necessary to attract "world-class" talent for its global retail rollout. Because the founders often hold significant equity, they are shielded from the "threat" of a spill resolution, as they can use their own voting power to defeat any motion to remove the board. This has led to a "governance stalemate" where strikes are recorded annually as a matter of ritual, but the board's core strategy remains unchanged.

## The Influence of Proxy Advisors and Institutional Conviction

The 2020–2023 period saw a marked increase in the influence of proxy advisory firms such as Glass Lewis, ISS, and Ownership Matters. These firms provide the research and voting recommendations that guide the vast majority of institutional capital in Australia.

A "second strike" is almost always preceded by a negative recommendation from at least two major proxy advisors. These firms have become increasingly stringent regarding "pay-performance alignment". In 2023, they were the primary drivers behind the "no" votes at ANZ and Fortescue, focusing on the use of undisclosed "strategic measures" in STI plans.

The rise of "coordinated investor conviction" is another emerging theme. Shareholders are no longer voting in isolation; they are increasingly engaging in "pre-AGM dialogues" to ensure their protest is heard. This has forced companies to be more proactive in their engagement strategies. As the data for 2024 shows, companies that engaged in "responsive" chair addresses and direct investor meetings prior to the AGM were significantly more likely to avoid a second strike than those who remained silent.

## Sector-Specific Governance Dynamics

The materials and technology sectors have consistently recorded higher-than-average strike rates from 2020 to 2023.

- **Materials (Mining and Resources):** These companies are subject to extreme commodity price cycles. Shareholders often perceive executive pay as being "leveraged to the market" rather than to individual performance. Furthermore, ESG issues—such as the destruction of cultural sites or safety fatalities—frequently bleed into the remuneration vote.
- **Information Technology:** This sector often faces strikes due to "maturation issues". Pay structures that were appropriate for a venture-backed startup (such as large, non-hurdled option grants) are frequently rejected once the company lists on the ASX 200 and attracts institutional investors who expect more rigour. Appen and BrainChip are prime examples of technology companies where "stalled commercial progress" led to repeated strikes.

## Future Outlook: The "Third Strike" and Beyond

As the Australian market moves beyond 2023, the emergence of "third," "fourth," and even "eighth" consecutive strikes suggests that the two-strikes rule may be reaching its limits as a deterrent for certain types of companies.

At the 2025 AGM, Dicker Data recorded a historic fifth consecutive strike with 76.6% against the report. BrainChip followed with its third consecutive strike. These cases demonstrate that for companies with strong founder control and a "resistant" philosophy, the two-strikes rule acts less as a corrective mechanism and more as a permanent indicator of a "governance gap".

However, for the broader market, the 2020–2023 data confirms that the rule has successfully forced a higher level of transparency. Boards are now more aware than ever that executive pay is a matter of public and regulatory interest. The integration of "malus" provisions, the tightening of LTI hurdles, and the move toward more detailed STI disclosures are all direct consequences of the pressure exerted by the two-strikes regime.

## Conclusion: Synthesising the 2020–2023 Experience

The analysis of remuneration second strikes from 2020 to 2023 reveals a corporate Australia that is increasingly under the microscope. The "two-strikes" rule has evolved from a novel legislative experiment into a cornerstone of the Australian governance landscape.

The core findings of this report—that TSR remains the primary driver of dissent, that board spills are rare but symbolic, and that transparency is the best defence against a strike—provide a clear roadmap for boards. The record-breaking levels of dissent in 2023, driven by a combination of high-profile corporate scandals and a challenging economic climate, suggest that the era of "passive" shareholder voting is over.

For institutional investors, the remuneration strike has become a sophisticated tool for enforcing a broader set of values, from safety and cybersecurity to regulatory compliance and social license. ## What a Spill Would Have Cost (Qantas-specific)

If a second strike at the 2024 AGM had triggered a successful spill resolution and spill meeting:

- **All 7 NEDs** (everyone except the Managing Director/CEO Hudson) would have ceased to hold office. At FY23 fee levels, that represents ~$2.18 million/year in board remuneration that would need to be re-established with replacement [directors.qantasnewsroom.com](http://directors.qantasnewsroom.com)+1
- **Recruitment costs** for replacing 7 ASX 50 board members simultaneously — at executive search fees of $100K–$300K per placement — would be in the range of **$700K–$2.1 million**.[[guerdonassociates](https://www.guerdonassociates.com/articles/a-review-of-2023-board-director-fee-adjustments/)]​
- **The CEO (Hudson) would be exempt** from the spill under the Corporations Act and would remain in [office.www8.austlii.edu](http://office.www8.austlii.edu)+1
- **Loss of institutional knowledge** during a period when Qantas was mid-way through fleet renewal ($4.3 billion program), ACCC settlement negotiations, and the governance review itself.

## The Voluntary Self-Sanction vs. Spill Exposure

The board's decision to **self-impose** a 33% NED fee cut (~$720K total) and claw back $9.26 million from Joyce was, in financial terms, more costly to the individuals involved than anything a spill meeting would have directly imposed. A spill doesn't reduce pay — it removes directors from office, who then lose *future* fees but keep everything already earned. The voluntary clawbacks targeted *past* remuneration that had already vested, which is a more severe financial consequence than a [spill.qantasnewsroom.com](http://spill.qantasnewsroom.com)+3

This is consistent with the broader pattern: boards facing a potential second strike often take **pre-emptive remedial action** (fee cuts, clawbacks, executive changes) precisely to avoid a spill vote, and investors typically accept these concessions rather than pursuing the nuclear option.guerdonassociates+1
