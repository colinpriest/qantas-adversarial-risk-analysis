# Corporate Governance and Regulatory Oversight in the ASX 100 and S&P 100: A Decadal Analysis (2013–2023)

The landscape of corporate governance within the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX) 100 and the US S&P 100 experienced a fundamental paradigm shift between July 1, 2013, and June 30, 2023. This decade was characterized by a transition from a "comply or explain" soft-law environment to a regime of intrusive, intensive regulatory supervision and mandated external remediation. Central to this evolution was the realization that formal compliance with governance checklists often masked profound cultural and behavioral failings.

## 3. Comparative Summary: USA vs. Australia

While both jurisdictions moved toward intensive supervision, the mechanisms of review differed. Australia employed broad, regulator-led sectoral "self-assessment" programs (e.g., APRA 36), while the US favored enforcement "sweeps" and individual criminal/civil monitorships.

## Commentary on Cross-Jurisdictional Findings

### Enforcement vs. Supervision

The data highlights a significant difference in the "tone" of external reviews. In the US, reviews are predominantly reactive and litigious, triggered by specific events such as the Boeing crashes or the Equifax breach. The "outcome" for US firms is often a settlement that mandates a new governance structure, such as Meta's Independent Privacy Committee. In contrast, Australian reviews—particularly the APRA 36 cohort—were prophylactic and systemic, seeking to identify "root cause immaturity" before a collapse occurred.

### The "Positive" Review Gap

Positive outcomes are more frequently documented in the US through third-party ratings like the "Average Director Weight" (ADW), which measures collective business acumen. In Australia, "Positive" ratings are rare in the regulatory sphere, as regulators such as APRA found that even entities with strong financial results often had "material weaknesses" in risk culture. The transition from Negative to Positive outcomes (as seen in Westpac's 12th report) requires sustained, independently verified remediation over multiple years.

### Divergence in Accountability

US courts have increasingly used the "Caremark" standard to hold boards accountable for "mission-critical" risks, a trend exemplified by the Boeing derivative litigation. Australia has followed a similar path, but through legislative accountability regimes like the Banking Executive Accountability Regime (BEAR) and the Financial Accountability Regime (FAR), which shift the burden to individuals to demonstrate they have taken "reasonable steps" to prevent failure.

## Conclusion: The Future of Governance Benchmarking

The 2013–2023 period demonstrates that corporate governance is no longer a static compliance checklist but a dynamic measure of organizational behavior. For the S&P 100, the focus remains on centralizing risk control to avoid the "silo effect" seen in major scandals. For the ASX 100, the imperative is "self-critical" assessment and the integration of non-financial risks into core strategic objectives. Both markets now face an era of "substantive oversight," where the strength of board leadership and the integrity of corporate culture are the ultimate determinants of institutional longevity.
