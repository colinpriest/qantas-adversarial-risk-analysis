# Corporate Governance and Regulatory Oversight in the ASX 100: A Decadal Analysis of External Reviews (2013–2023)

The landscape of corporate governance within the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX) 100 experienced a fundamental paradigm shift between July 1, 2013, and June 30, 2023. This decade was characterized by a transition from a "comply or explain" soft-law environment to a regime of intrusive, intensive regulatory supervision and mandated external remediation. Central to this evolution was the realization by both regulators and the public that formal compliance with governance checklists often masked profound cultural and behavioral failings. The period began with the relatively stable application of the 3rd Edition of the ASX Corporate Governance Principles and Recommendations and culminated in an era defined by Royal Commissions, prudential inquiries, and multi-year enforceable undertakings that fundamentally redefined the "social license to operate" for Australia’s largest listed entities.

## The Evolution of Governance Supervision in Australia

At the inception of the review period in mid-2013, Australian corporate governance was largely viewed through the lens of financial risk and shareholder rights. The 2014 release of the 3rd Edition of the ASX Principles introduced broader considerations for risk, but it was not until the latter half of the decade that non-financial risks—such as culture, conduct, and community impact—became the primary focus of external governance reviews. This shift was catalyzed by a series of systemic failures in the financial services and gaming sectors, which demonstrated that technical legal compliance could coexist with unethical behavior and systemic misconduct.

The regulatory architecture supporting these reviews involves a tripartite structure: the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC), which enforces the Corporations Act 2001; the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA), which oversees the stability and governance of financial institutions; and the Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre (AUSTRAC), focused on anti-money laundering. Throughout the decade, these bodies moved toward a "Why not litigate?" posture, employing external reviews as both a diagnostic tool and a corrective mechanism.

## The Catalyst: The 2018 APRA Prudential Inquiry into CBA

The most significant turning point in the decadal history of governance reviews was the 2018 APRA Prudential Inquiry into the Commonwealth Bank of Australia (CBA). This review was initiated following several high-profile incidents, including the AUSTRAC money-laundering case and the widespread mis-selling of financial products. The inquiry was unique in its breadth, focusing not on financial metrics, but on the "cultural, governance, and accountability" frameworks that allowed such issues to persist.

The findings of the CBA inquiry were stark and comprehensively negative. The inquiry identified a "sense of complacency," a "lack of curiosity," and a "tone at the top" that did not effectively cascade through the organization. Specifically, the board and senior leadership were criticized for their failure to provide adequate oversight of non-financial risks, a theme that would reverberate through the rest of the ASX 100 for the following five years.

The immediate outcome for CBA was the imposition of a $\$1$ billion operational risk capital add-on and the requirement to enter into a court-enforceable undertaking to implement 35 recommendations. This review effectively created a new blueprint for regulatory supervision in Australia, shifting the burden of proof from the regulator to the entity to demonstrate its "cultural fitness".

## The APRA 36 Self-Assessment Program: A Cross-Industry Governance Audit

Following the CBA inquiry, APRA extended the scrutiny to 36 of the nation’s largest financial institutions, including the majority of the ASX 100 banks and insurers. This program, initiated in mid-2018, required these institutions to conduct a board-led self-assessment against the findings of the CBA report. The result was the most extensive collection of governance data in Australian history.

### Themes and Outcomes of the Self-Assessments

The results of the 36 self-assessments, analyzed in a May 2019 APRA report, were broadly negative. APRA identified three primary areas of weakness across the sector:

1. **Management of Non-Financial Risk:** Most entities struggled to define and monitor risks related to conduct and culture as rigorously as they did for credit or market risk.
2. **Accountability and Remuneration:** There was a persistent failure to link executive remuneration to risk outcomes. The reviews found that "malus" (the clawing back of bonuses) was rarely used, even in the face of significant compliance failures.
3. **Governance Maturity:** APRA noted a "wide variation" in the quality of the assessments. While some boards were deeply engaged, others took a "tick-the-box" approach, justifying their performance by claiming the issues at CBA were not applicable to their specific business models.

## Case Study: Westpac and the Promontory Independent Review

Westpac Banking Corporation’s governance review period represents the most protracted and intensive remediation effort of the decade. Triggered by the 2019 AUSTRAC litigation and subsequent systemic risk governance failures, Westpac entered into an Enforceable Undertaking (EU) with APRA in December 2020.

The EU mandated the "Customer Outcomes and Risk Excellence" (CORE) program, which was overseen by an independent reviewer, Promontory Australia. Over 30 months, Promontory released 12 quarterly reports assessing Westpac’s progress across 19 workstreams and 354 distinct activities.

Initially, the review was highly negative, highlighting deep-seated "root causes" of failure, including a complex and fragmented organizational structure that blurred accountabilities. However, as the CORE program progressed, the outcome shifted toward a positive rating. By the 12th report in May 2024, Promontory confirmed that Westpac had successfully completed its Integrated Plan, resulting in a "substantial improvement" in risk governance and a culture where risk is now more actively managed. This case study serves as a benchmark for how negative regulatory reviews can, through sustained effort and independent oversight, lead to a positive structural transformation.

## The Gaming Sector: Crown Resorts and Star Entertainment

The governance reviews of Crown Resorts (CWN) and The Star Entertainment Group (SGR) between 2020 and 2023 represent the most severe negative outcomes for ASX 100 entities during the decade. Unlike the financial sector reviews, which sought to improve existing entities, the gaming sector inquiries focused on the threshold question of "suitability" to hold a casino license.

### The Bergin Inquiry and Crown Resorts

The 2021 Bergin Inquiry into Crown Resorts identified "disgraceful" conduct, including the facilitation of money laundering through subsidiary accounts and continued relationships with junket operators linked to organized crime. The review was comprehensively negative, identifying "significant failures" of risk management and a board that was "ineffective" due to the influence of a major shareholder.

The governance failures were attributed to several factors:

- **Information Silos:** Critical information about AML risks was not escalated to the board.
- **Conflicts of Interest:** Directors were found to have loyalties that conflicted with their duties to the company and the regulator.
- **Profit Maximization over Compliance:** The culture was described as "recklessly indifferent" to the risk of criminal infiltration.

The outcome was a total loss of "suitability," leading to the appointment of special managers in Victoria and Western Australia and a complete board renewal.

### The Star Entertainment Transformation

Following the Crown inquiries, The Star Entertainment Group underwent similar scrutiny through the Bell and Gotterson inquiries. These reviews mirrored the Crown findings, identifying systemic AML failures and poor governance. By October 2025, The Star was still in a "transformation program" aimed at regaining its suitability, with reviews noting a statutorily net loss of $\$427.9$ million due to fines, penalties, and impairment costs associated with these governance failures. The reviews here remain negative, as the company struggles with "operational, regulatory, and financial obstacles" to sustainability.

## The Resource Sector: Rio Tinto and the Social License

The 2020 Board Review of Cultural Heritage Management at Rio Tinto Limited provides a critical example of a non-regulatory review (though influenced by parliamentary pressure) with a highly negative outcome. The destruction of the Juukan Gorge rock shelters was not a breach of Western Australian law at the time, but the subsequent board review identified it as a catastrophic failure of corporate governance.

The review found that Rio Tinto’s internal systems for escalating heritage risks to the senior executive level were flawed. It identified a "work culture" that was more focused on securing legal approvals than on managing the "social performance" and community expectations that underpin a social license to operate.

The causal outcome of this review was a revolution in executive accountability. For the first time in the ASX 100, a CEO and several top executives were forced to resign despite not having broken any laws, simply because the governance failures led to an "unacceptable" social outcome. This has led to the adoption of "co-management" models with Indigenous communities across the sector, shifting the governance of "social performance" from a back-office function to a core board responsibility.

## Technology Governance: The ASX Group Inquiries

The governance of technology and operational risk became a central focus for the market operator itself, ASX Limited. In September 2018, ASIC released Report 592, which outlined the findings of a review into the technology governance and operational risk management of the ASX Group following market outages in 2016.

The review was negative, identifying that while the ASX had robust technical systems, its *governance* of those systems—specifically the escalation of technical risks to the board—needed significant improvement. This was compounded by the subsequent failure of the CHESS replacement project, which led to a 2025 inquiry by ASIC focusing on governance, capability, and risk management frameworks. The interim report from this inquiry identified "key underlying drivers of concern," leading to commitments from the ASX for a "transformational package" of reforms. This demonstrates that technology is now viewed as a primary governance risk for the systemic stability of the Australian financial market.

## The Modern Slavery Act and Governance Disclosure Ratings

In the final years of the decadal period, external "reviews" of governance expanded to include quantitative and qualitative ratings of environmental and social disclosures. The Modern Slavery Act 2018 (Cth) mandated that ASX 100 entities report on supply chain risks, which triggered a wave of external audits by academic and professional organizations.

Between 2020 and 2023, organizations like Monash University’s Modern Slavery Research Team and the UNSW Digital Sustainability Hub provided "external reviews" of these governance disclosures.

## Determinants of Review Outcomes: Narrative Synthesis

The outcomes of these governance reviews—whether negative, neutral, or positive—were determined by four consistent factors:

### 1. Board Curiosity and Challenge

Reviews of CBA, Crown, and Rio Tinto all identified a "lack of curiosity" as a primary driver of failure. A negative rating was almost guaranteed when a board accepted management reports at face value without challenging the "underlying drivers" of non-financial risk. In contrast, the positive outcome at BOQ in 2020 was attributed to the board proactively identifying the need for renewal *before* a regulatory crisis.

### 2. The "Tone at the Top" vs. the "Echo from the Bottom"

Regulatory reviews consistently looked for evidence that the board’s stated values were reflected in frontline behavior. The APRA self-assessments found that while boards often had a positive "view" of their own performance, this did not match the "stark realities" of conduct in the branches or on the casino floor.

### 3. Accountability and the "Should We" Question

The CBA inquiry introduced the "Should We?" question—shifting the focus from what is *legally permissible* to what is *ethically right* for the customer or community. Negative review outcomes were common for entities that focused strictly on the "Could We?" (legal) dimension, as seen in the Juukan Gorge destruction.

### 4. Remuneration as a Governance Signal

Reviewers like APRA and Promontory heavily scrutinized whether remuneration frameworks "incentivized delivery of sound outcomes". The persistent failure to apply financial consequences to executives for risk failures was a hallmark of the negative reviews throughout the decade.

## The Convergence of ESG and Governance in the ASX 100

By the end of the period in June 2023, the distinction between "governance" and "ESG" had largely disappeared. External reviews of ASX 100 companies now routinely integrate climate risk, social impact, and diversity into the definition of "good governance".

## Summary of Findings and Causal Implications

The decadal data reveals that governance is no longer a static set of rules but a dynamic, behavior-driven requirement. The causal relationships identified across the ASX 100 demonstrate that:

- **Complacency leads to Regulation:** Companies that failed to conduct rigorous internal reviews (like CBA and Crown) eventually faced much more invasive and expensive regulatory inquiries.
- **Remediation requires Independence:** The transition from a negative to a positive review outcome (as seen with Westpac) requires an independent, external reviewer to validate the "effectiveness" of change.
- **Non-Financial Risk is the New Frontier:** The majority of external reviews in the latter half of the decade focused on culture, AML, technology, and social license, rather than traditional financial stability.

In conclusion, the 2013–2023 period saw the "hollowing out" of tick-the-box governance. The ASX 100 constituents that survived and thrived were those that embraced "self-critical" assessments and integrated non-financial risk management into their core strategic objectives. The shift toward mandated external oversight—through EUs and independent reviewers—likely represents a permanent change in the Australian regulatory landscape, ensuring that boards are held accountable not just for what they achieve, but *how* they achieve it.
