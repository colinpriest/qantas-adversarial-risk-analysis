# Analysis of CEO Retention and Turnover Following Major Corporate Crises

The following analysis estimates the "survival rate" of S&P/ASX 100 CEOs when their tenure is marked by a major institutional crisis (governance failure, fraud, regulatory enforcement, or extreme shareholder pressure). Based on a sample of 26 high-profile crisis events identified in the research period, the estimated **CEO Departure Rate** following a major crisis is **73.1%**.

### Accompanying Commentary

**1. The "Royal Commission" Effect on Tenure**
The Financial Services Royal Commission (2018–2019) remains the most significant driver of "forced" CEO turnover in the ASX 100. It created an environment where the "stewardship" model of leadership was insufficient to protect executives from personal accountability. While CEOs like Andrew Thorburn (NAB) and Craig Meller (AMP) departed immediately following damaging testimony or reports, others like Shayne Elliott (ANZ) survived by proactively leading the remediation efforts and accepting symbolic bonus cuts.

**2. Regulatory Survival vs. Moral Failure**
Research indicates a sharp divergence in survival rates based on the *type* of crisis. "Moral" or "Conduct" failures (e.g., QBE's Pat Regan or James Hardie's Jack Truong) almost invariably lead to immediate departure, often with the board explicitly citing a breach of the Code of Ethics. Conversely, "Operational" crises (e.g., data breaches at Medibank or terminal outages at Tyro) allow for a longer "remediation runway" where the CEO is often retained to provide stability during the technical recovery phase.

**3. The Role of Contrition and Bonus Forfeiture**
CEOs who "stay" following a crisis typically employ a specific governance strategy: the unreserved public apology combined with the voluntary forfeiture of bonuses. Brad Banducci (Woolworths) and Don Meij (Domino's) both retained their roles after multi-million dollar underpayment scandals by forgoing their Short-Term Incentives (STIs) to demonstrate accountability without vacating the helm.

**4. Lagged vs. Immediate Market Reaction**
Consistent with academic findings, Australian boards tend toward a "lagged effect" for turnover in performance-related crises compared to their US counterparts. For instance, Michael Clarke (TWE) and Steve McCann (Lendlease) oversaw periods of strategic difficulty where their eventual departures were managed as "transitions" despite clear underlying pressure from institutional shareholders following negative Cumulative Abnormal Returns (CAR).
