# **CEO survival after crises:** integrity failures vs operational failures

Academic work does support an important distinction between **integrity/moral failures** and **competence/operational failures**, but it is more nuanced than “almost invariably immediate departure,” and there is little that is Australia‑specific.

### Integrity (moral / conduct) vs competence (operational) failures

Several studies explicitly separate **integrity (ethical)** from **competence (performance/operational)** failures and examine CEO consequences:

- A policy‑capturing experiment with investors shows they strongly distinguish **integrity failures** (e.g., fraud, unethical conduct) from **competence failures** (e.g., strategic or performance mistakes). After an integrity failure, investors are particularly negative about keeping an insider CEO and prefer an outside or interim successor, indicating strong pressure for visible leadership change following moral lapses (Connelly et al., 2016).
- Research on **misconduct and CEO turnover** finds that misconduct events significantly increase the probability of CEO dismissal, but the likelihood depends on firm‑level characteristics (e.g., identity orientation, stakeholder orientation) and external pressure, not every case ends in removal (Çolak et al., 2023; Farro et al., 2021).
- A global study of **ESG‑related corporate misbehavior** finds CEO turnover risk increases markedly when media coverage of stakeholder‑related incidents reaches “extreme” levels, even when stock prices do not decline, consistent with boards responding to reputational and moral dimensions of crises, not just financial performance (Çolak et al., 2023).

These findings line up with the idea that **ethical/moral scandals are more likely to trigger CEO exits and to require a “symbolic reset”** (often via outside successors or explicit ethics framing), whereas **competence/operational failures** give boards more discretion to retain the CEO, especially if technical remediation and continuity are valued.

### Operational / technical crises (e.g., data breaches)

Work on technology and security failures suggests a **less automatic link** to CEO removal:

- For information security breaches, CEO turnover risk increases, but it is sensitive to the *type* of breach and often falls more heavily on executives directly responsible for IT (CIO/CTO), not always the CEO (Banker & Feng, 2019; Say & Vasudeva, 2020).
- Data breaches tend to trigger **targeted management changes and structural responses** (e.g., CTO turnover, unrelated divestitures), which can be more “learning” or symbolic responses rather than automatic top‑CEO removal (Banker & Feng, 2019; Say & Vasudeva, 2020).

This aligns with your “remediation runway” intuition: in operational crises, boards frequently seek stability and technical problem‑solving before considering CEO replacement.

### CEO turnover reasons and coding, including misconduct

- Large‑scale turnover databases for S&P 1500 CEOs code detailed **departure reasons**, including ethical or legal problems vs performance‑related or voluntary exits (Gentry et al., 2021). These show that **involuntary dismissals tied to misconduct are a minority of all exits but carry strong negative signaling**, often accompanied by narrative references to misconduct or governance breakdowns.
- Reviews of CEO turnover more broadly emphasize multiple clusters of causes (organizational, performance, and personal), with **ethical/misconduct issues embedded in the “performance/organizational” categories**, not treated as universal triggers of immediate exit (Mathew et al., 2024; Hermes et al., 2025).

### Evidence gaps and Australia

- Existing quantitative studies and open datasets are U.S. or global; none in this set provide **systematic, Australia‑only estimates** of CEO survival by “moral vs operational” crisis type.
- Australian‑based authors contribute to the CEO‑turnover literature, but the empirical settings in these papers and databases are primarily U.S. listed firms (Gentry et al., 2021; Mathew et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2020).

### How this compares to your quoted claim

- There is **strong support** for:
  - Higher CEO dismissal risk after **integrity/moral misconduct**,
  - Strong investor preference for visible leadership change in such cases, and
  - Greater board discretion and more varied responses after **operational/technical crises**.
- There is **not clear quantitative evidence** that moral failures “almost invariably” lead to immediate CEO departure across large samples; outcomes vary with media intensity, governance, firm identity, and country context (Çolak et al., 2023; Farro et al., 2021).

To test the exact Australian claim rigorously, one would need to build an ASX‑wide event–study dataset (classifying crises as integrity vs operational) using a turnover‑coding approach similar to Gentry et al.’s S&P 1500 database (Gentry et al., 2021).
