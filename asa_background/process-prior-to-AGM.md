ASA describes its model as **engagement-first** and notes that its volunteer company monitors “engage with companies prior to their AGM” as part of forming Voting Intentions and then attend AGMs to vote proxies and ask questions. Their voting and engagement guidelines emphasise that the monitor’s job is to reach a considered recommendation on the remuneration report, with engagement around remuneration structures and any strikes forming a major part of that work. [australianshareholders.com](https://www.australianshareholders.com.au/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/ASA-voting-guidelines_2024.pdf)

More broadly, ASIC’s review of proxy adviser practices found that, in 65 out of 80 cases where a proxy adviser issued an “against” recommendation, there had been engagement with the company beforehand. While that ASIC work focused on ISS/Glass Lewis/Ownership Matters, it reflects the general norm in the Australian market: shareholder bodies and proxy advisers typically discuss concerns with issuers in the weeks before finalising a negative recommendation on remuneration. [guerdonassociates](https://www.guerdonassociates.com/articles/asic-reports-on-proxy-adviser-engagement-practices-the-final-one-for-now/)

So for ASA in particular, it is **normal and expected** that there would be conversations with the company in the lead‑up to a recommendation to vote against the remuneration report, rather than the “against” call appearing out of the blue. [australianshareholders.com](https://www.australianshareholders.com.au/company-monitoring-training-modules/understanding-the-remuneration-framework-part-2/)

ASA itself says that, as a rule, it is engaging with companies **well before** Voting Intentions are published, and at least weeks (often months) ahead of the AGM.

A few concrete anchors:

- **ASA Voting Intentions timing:** ASA’s own description of its monitoring work states that Voting Intentions and AGM reports “are available two weeks before the meeting and a week after the meeting, respectively.” That implies the *public* recommendation (e.g., “Against” remuneration) typically appears about **14 days before the AGM**. [australianshareholders.com](https://www.australianshareholders.com.au/companies-we-monitor/)
- **Pre‑AGM engagement expectation:** ASA guidance on preparing for pre‑AGM meetings tells monitors to “reach out early to the company’s investor relations or company secretary to let them know you will need time with the chair or appropriate NEDs before the AGM so you can decide how you intend to vote on AGM resolutions,” and to “keep track of what the company is doing throughout the year so when you are close to the AGM, you know what to ask about.” This clearly envisages **engagement starting weeks to months before the AGM**, not days. [storage.googleapis](https://storage.googleapis.com/chr-asa-staging/uploads/2023/08/Preparing_for_the_PreAGM.pdf)
- **ASA company-monitor model:** ASA’s submission to Treasury notes that company monitors “engage with companies prior to their AGM,” and highlights that most AGMs for June‑year companies occur 4–5 months after year‑end. Combined with the 28‑day minimum notice-of-meeting period, this suggests a **natural engagement window of several weeks** between the notice of meeting going out and the AGM itself, layered on top of any earlier, ongoing contact. [treasury.gov](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2021-12/c2021-169360-asa.pdf)
- **Market practice for proxy engagement:** ASIC’s review of proxy adviser engagement practices found that, for mainstream proxy advisers, most engagement on contentious AGM items occurs between **September and October**, ahead of the October–November AGM peak. Law firms and IR advisers similarly recommend checking in with key shareholders and proxy advisers “ahead of time” on sensitive issues like remuneration, often in **June–July** for October–November AGMs. [piperalderman.com](https://piperalderman.com.au/insight/charting-a-strategic-path-to-agm-success/)
